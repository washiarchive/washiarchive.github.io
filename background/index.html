Background: Washi & Production Techniques

Washi is a family of traditional Japanese handmade papers valued for strength, translucency, and tactile character. The Digital Washi Archive supports research and conservation by pairing macro images with standardized metadata, making materials easier to compare and cite.

Fibers

Most historic washi uses bast fibers from three plants:

  • Kozo (paper mulberry): long fibers, excellent strength; common for general papers and mounting.
  • Gampi: naturally lustrous, short/slippery fibers; yields crisp, ink-friendly sheets.
  • Mitsumata: soft, fine fibers; smooth printing and drawing surfaces.

Sheet formation (with neri)

Cooked, cleaned fibers are beaten and suspended in water with neri (a plant-based formation aid). The vatman lifts a screen (su) inside a frame (keta), rocking to deposit thin layers. Neri slows drainage and helps even fiber distribution, reducing clumps and edge build-up.

Pressing & drying

Fresh sheets are couched into a stack and pressed to expel water. Drying may be on boards, plaster walls, or heated surfaces; each route affects surface gloss and fiber lay. Sizing or surface treatments can further tune ink behavior and durability.

Why macro imaging?

At macro scale, fiber length, ribboning, vessel elements, inclusions, and sheet topology become legible. These features, combined with metadata fields (fiber type, region, period/maker, intended use, rights) aid attribution, matching for repairs, and reproducible scholarship.
